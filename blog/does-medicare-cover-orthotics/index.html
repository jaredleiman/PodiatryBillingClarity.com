Does Medicare Cover Orthotics? What Podiatrists and Patients Need to Know

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

Medicare's coverage of orthotics is more limited than most patients expect. Medicare Part B covers custom-molded orthotic inserts primarily through the Therapeutic Shoe Program (TDSP) for patients with diabetes who meet specific qualifying conditions. Standard over-the-counter orthotics, arch supports, and comfort insoles are not covered. Understanding the specific coverage rules is essential for podiatrists who prescribe orthotics and for patients who want to know what Medicare will pay for.

The Therapeutic Shoe Program (TDSP)

The primary pathway for Medicare orthotic coverage is the Therapeutic Shoe Program, also known as the Diabetic Shoe Bill. Under TDSP, Medicare covers one pair of therapeutic shoes (depth shoes or custom-molded shoes) per calendar year plus three pairs of custom-molded or heat-molded inserts per calendar year (or two pairs of inserts plus shoe modifications).

Who Qualifies

To qualify for TDSP, the patient must have diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2), have one or more of the following qualifying conditions: peripheral neuropathy with callus formation, history of pre-ulcerative calluses, history of previous ulceration, foot deformity, previous amputation (partial or complete), or poor circulation. The patient's treating physician (not the podiatrist who dispenses the shoes/orthotics) must certify the diabetes diagnosis and the qualifying condition, and the podiatrist or other qualified provider must perform a comprehensive foot exam and document the need for therapeutic footwear.

Critical billing point: The certifying physician (who confirms the diabetes diagnosis and qualifying condition) cannot be the same provider who dispenses the shoes and orthotics. If you're the podiatrist dispensing the shoes, the patient's PCP, endocrinologist, or other treating physician must sign the certification statement.

What Medicare Pays

Medicare pays 80% of the allowed amount for TDSP items after the Part B deductible. The patient pays 20% coinsurance. Approximate allowed amounts include depth shoes at 20-50 per pair, custom-molded shoes at 50-00 per pair, and custom-molded inserts at 0-0 per pair. The items must be obtained from a Medicare-enrolled DME supplier. Many podiatrists obtain DME supplier certification to dispense shoes and orthotics directly from their offices.

Custom Foot Orthotics Outside TDSP

For non-diabetic patients, Medicare coverage of custom foot orthotics is very limited. Medicare Part B generally does not cover custom arch supports, orthotic insoles for plantar fasciitis or flat feet, over-the-counter inserts, or shoe modifications for non-diabetic conditions. Some Medicare Advantage plans offer expanded orthotic benefits beyond what original Medicare covers. Always verify the specific MA plan's DME/orthotic benefits.

AFOs and Other Bracing

Medicare does cover ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) and other lower extremity braces when they are medically necessary for conditions like drop foot, post-surgical stabilization, and neuromuscular conditions affecting gait. These are billed under different HCPCS codes (L-codes) than foot orthotics and have separate coverage criteria and documentation requirements.

Documentation Requirements for TDSP

Successful TDSP claims require a physician certification statement signed by the treating physician (not the dispensing provider), a comprehensive diabetic foot exam documented by the dispensing provider, documentation of the qualifying condition with clinical findings, a detailed description of the items dispensed, and proof that the items were delivered to the patient.

Missing or incomplete documentation — particularly the physician certification — is the #1 reason for TDSP claim denials. Create a standardized workflow that obtains the certification before ordering the shoes/orthotics.

Common Billing Errors

  • Same provider certifying and dispensing — The certifying physician and the dispensing provider must be different practitioners
  • Missing qualifying condition — Diabetes alone doesn't qualify — the patient must have at least one of the listed secondary conditions
  • Exceeding annual limits — One pair of shoes and three pairs of inserts per calendar year is the maximum
  • Billing without DME supplier number — The dispensing provider or supplier must have a DME supplier number to bill Medicare for shoes and orthotics
  • Dispensing before certification — The physician certification must be completed before or at the time of dispensing

The Bottom Line

Medicare orthotic coverage is narrow but valuable for the right patients. The Therapeutic Shoe Program covers diabetic patients with qualifying conditions, and when billed correctly, it provides a meaningful benefit that protects vulnerable feet and prevents amputations. For non-diabetic patients, custom orthotics are generally out-of-pocket expenses unless covered by supplemental or Medicare Advantage plans. For podiatrists, mastering TDSP documentation and billing is worth the effort — it serves patients who need the benefit most and generates consistent DME revenue.
