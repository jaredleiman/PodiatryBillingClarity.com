CPT Code 11720: Nail Trimming and Debridement (1-5 Nails) Billing Guide

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 6 min read

CPT 11720 covers the debridement of nails, any method, 1 to 5 nails. It is the counterpart to 11721 (6+ nails) and is the appropriate code when fewer than 6 nails are debrided during a single encounter. Despite its lower reimbursement compared to 11721, 11720 is essential for accurate billing — using 11721 when fewer than 6 nails were treated constitutes upcoding.

When to Bill 11720

Bill 11720 when you debride 1 to 5 nails during the visit. The debridement must involve reducing thickened, mycotic, dystrophic, or otherwise abnormal nails that require professional skill — not simple cosmetic nail trimming. Common scenarios include debriding mycotic great toenails bilaterally (2 nails = 11720), treating 3-4 dystrophic nails on one foot, and reducing a single significantly thickened or incurvated nail.

11720 vs. 11721: The Nail Count Threshold

The only difference is the number: 11720 for 1-5 nails, 11721 for 6 or more. These codes are mutually exclusive — bill one or the other, never both. If you debride 5 nails, bill 11720. If you debride 6, bill 11721. There is no rounding — the count must be exact and documented.

Why this matters for audits: A practice that almost never bills 11720 — billing 11721 for virtually every nail visit — signals to auditors that nail counts may be inflated. A clinically realistic billing pattern includes a healthy mix of both codes, reflecting that not every patient needs all nails debrided at every visit.

Medicare Coverage

Like 11721, nail debridement under 11720 is classified as routine foot care and requires a Class Finding for Medicare coverage. The patient must have a documented systemic condition (diabetes with neuropathy, PVD, etc.) that makes professional nail care medically necessary. The appropriate Q modifier (Q7, Q8, or Q9) must be appended to the claim. Without the Class Finding and Q modifier, the claim will be denied.

Documentation Requirements

Your note must include the exact number of nails debrided (1-5), which specific nails were treated (identify by toe and foot), the condition of each nail (thickened, mycotic, dystrophic — describe specifically), the technique used, and the Class Finding with systemic condition and foot manifestation for Medicare patients.

Common Billing Errors

  • Billing 11721 when only 1-5 nails were debrided — Upcoding; bill 11720
  • Billing 11720 twice — You cannot bill two units of 11720 to represent nails on different feet. 11720 is per encounter regardless of how many feet are involved.
  • Not documenting nail count — "Nails debrided" without specifying how many won't support either code
  • Billing nail debridement on a nail that was also avulsed — If you remove a nail surgically (11730), you can't also bill debridement on that same nail

The Bottom Line

11720 is straightforward — 1 to 5 nails debrided, with a documented count and Class Finding for Medicare. Using it accurately alongside 11721 creates the billing pattern that reflects clinical reality and passes audit review. Count the nails, document the nails, support with a Class Finding, and bill the code that matches the count.
