How Much Does a Podiatrist Cost? With and Without Insurance

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

A visit to the podiatrist typically costs $100 to $250 without insurance for a standard office visit, and $20 to $75 with insurance through copays or coinsurance. The actual cost depends on the type of visit, the services performed, and your insurance coverage.

Cost Without Insurance

Self-pay podiatrist costs vary by visit type. An initial consultation with examination typically runs $100-$200. Follow-up visits for routine foot care (nail trimming, callus removal) cost $75-$150. Visits involving minor procedures (ingrown toenail removal, wart treatment) range from $150-$350. X-rays add $50-$100 to the visit cost. Custom orthotics typically cost $200-$500 on top of the fitting appointment.

Costs also vary by geography — podiatrists in major metropolitan areas charge 30-50% more than those in smaller markets due to higher overhead costs.

Cost With Insurance

Most health insurance plans cover podiatry as a specialist visit. Your cost depends on your plan type. With a PPO plan, expect a specialist copay of $30-$75 per visit. HMO plans typically require a referral from your PCP and charge $20-$50 copays. High-deductible plans mean you pay the full negotiated rate until your deductible is met, which could be $100-$200 per visit. Medicare covers medically necessary podiatry at 80% after the Part B deductible, with the patient paying 20% coinsurance (typically $10-$30 per visit for routine care).

Important distinction: Most health insurance plans cover podiatry visits for medical conditions (infections, injuries, diabetic foot care, surgical needs). Some plans do not cover routine foot care (nail trimming, callus removal) unless you have a qualifying medical condition. Always verify what your specific plan covers before your visit.

What Affects the Price

  • Visit type — A simple nail trimming costs less than a surgical procedure. The CPT codes billed determine the total charge.
  • Number of services — A visit with E/M, X-rays, and a procedure costs more than a single service visit.
  • Location — Urban practices have higher overhead and charge accordingly.
  • Provider experience — Podiatrists with specialized training (sports medicine, wound care, reconstructive surgery) may charge higher rates.
  • Facility type — Hospital-based podiatry clinics often charge facility fees on top of the professional fee, making visits significantly more expensive than private practice visits.

How to Save on Podiatry Costs

  • Ask about cash-pay rates — Many podiatry practices offer self-pay discounts of 15-30%
  • Use your HSA/FSA — Podiatry visits are eligible expenses
  • Choose a private practice over a hospital clinic — Hospital outpatient departments add facility fees that can double the cost
  • Ask if your condition qualifies for medical coverage — Diabetic foot care, infections, and injuries are typically covered by health insurance even if routine foot care is not
  • Compare prices — Call 2-3 practices in your area for self-pay pricing

What Medicare Patients Pay

For Medicare patients, podiatry costs are among the most affordable specialist visits — if your condition qualifies for coverage. Medicare covers medical foot care (injuries, infections, diabetic care, surgical procedures) at 80% after the deductible, with the patient paying about $10-$30 per visit. Routine foot care (nail trimming, callus removal) is covered only for patients with qualifying systemic conditions (diabetes with neuropathy, PVD). Without a qualifying condition, routine care must be paid out of pocket.

The Bottom Line

Podiatrist costs are predictable and manageable — especially with insurance or for patients who qualify for Medicare coverage of routine foot care. The key is understanding whether your visit will be classified as medical (usually covered) or routine (covered only with a qualifying condition), and asking about self-pay options if you're paying out of pocket.
