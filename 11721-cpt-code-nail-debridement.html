CPT Code 11721: Nail Debridement (6 or More Nails) Billing Guide

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

CPT 11721 covers the debridement (trimming, reducing, or removing) of nails — any method, 6 or more nails. It is one of the highest-volume codes in podiatry and one of the most frequently audited. The difference between 11720 (1-5 nails) and 11721 (6+ nails) is purely the number of nails debrided, but the reimbursement difference is significant — making accurate nail count documentation essential.

When to Use 11721

Bill 11721 when you debride 6 or more nails during a single encounter. The debridement must go beyond simple trimming — it involves reducing thickened, mycotic, or dystrophic nails that require professional skill to manage safely. The medical necessity for professional nail debridement (rather than self-care nail trimming) is established through the patient's systemic condition and the condition of the nails themselves.

11721 vs. 11720

11720 covers debridement of 1-5 nails. 11721 covers debridement of 6 or more nails. These codes cannot be billed together — you use one or the other based on the total number of nails debrided. If you debride 5 nails, bill 11720. If you debride 6, bill 11721. The cutoff is exact — no rounding up.

Audit focus: The OIG has specifically flagged practices that bill 11721 on nearly every encounter. A practice that never bills 11720 — meaning every patient supposedly needs 6+ nails debrided every visit — will trigger review. Your billing pattern should reflect clinical reality: some patients need all nails debrided, others need only a few.

Medicare Coverage Requirements

For Medicare patients, nail debridement (both 11720 and 11721) is classified as routine foot care. This means it is only covered when the patient has a documented Class Finding — a systemic condition (diabetes, PVD, neuropathy) that creates a hazard if a non-professional performed the care. Without the Class Finding, Medicare will deny the claim regardless of how dystrophic the nails are.

The appropriate Q modifier (Q7, Q8, or Q9) must be appended to the claim to indicate the qualifying systemic condition.

Documentation Requirements

Every 11721 claim must include the exact number of nails debrided (must be 6 or more), which nails were debrided (identify by toe — e.g., "bilateral great toenails, bilateral 2nd-5th toenails, 10 nails total"), the condition of the nails (thickened, mycotic, dystrophic, incurvated — describe specifically), the debridement technique used, the Class Finding documentation linking the patient's systemic condition to the need for professional care, and the Q modifier on the claim.

A compliant note: "Debrided 8 nails — bilateral hallux, bilateral 2nd, 3rd, and 4th toenails. All nails thickened (2-3mm), mycotic with subungual debris. Patient has Type 2 DM with documented peripheral neuropathy (class A finding). Professional debridement required due to loss of protective sensation — self-care would create risk of skin breakdown and infection. Q7 modifier applied."

Frequency Considerations

Medicare does not specify a maximum frequency for nail debridement, but visits more frequent than every 60 days will draw scrutiny. Most MACs consider 60-day intervals to be the standard for routine nail debridement. Visits more frequently than every 60 days require additional documentation explaining why the shorter interval is medically necessary — for example, rapidly growing mycotic nails causing pain or skin breakdown.

Common Billing Errors

  • Billing 11721 when fewer than 6 nails were debrided — This is the most common error and constitutes upcoding
  • Not documenting the exact nail count — "Nails debrided bilaterally" without specifying the count won't support either code level
  • Missing Class Finding for Medicare patients — No Class Finding = automatic denial for routine foot care
  • Billing 11721 with 11720 — These codes are mutually exclusive per encounter
  • Billing nail debridement with nail avulsion (11730) on the same nail — If you avulse a nail, you cannot also bill debridement on that same nail

The Bottom Line

11721 is straightforward in concept — 6 or more nails debrided — but the documentation requirements make it a compliance-sensitive code. Count every nail, document every nail, support with a Class Finding for Medicare patients, and use the correct Q modifier. A clean 11721 billing pattern with thorough documentation is the strongest protection against the audit scrutiny this code receives.
