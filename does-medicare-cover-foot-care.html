Does Medicare Cover Foot Care? Routine vs. Medical Explained

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

Medicare covers foot care — but the type of care determines whether you pay nothing or everything. Medical foot care (treating injuries, infections, and diseases of the foot) is covered like any other Medicare Part B service. Routine foot care (nail trimming, callus removal) is excluded unless you have a qualifying systemic condition that makes professional care medically necessary.

Medical Foot Care: Always Covered

Medicare covers the diagnosis and treatment of medical foot conditions without restrictions. This includes foot fractures and injuries, infections (cellulitis, osteomyelitis, fungal infections causing secondary complications), diabetic foot ulcers and wound care, bunions, hammertoes, and other structural deformities requiring treatment, plantar fasciitis and other inflammatory conditions, peripheral neuropathy evaluation and management, and surgical procedures (nail avulsion, bunionectomy, amputation).

For these medical conditions, Medicare pays 80% of the allowed amount after the Part B deductible. The patient pays 20% coinsurance. No special modifiers or Class Findings are required — standard medical necessity documentation applies.

Routine Foot Care: Covered Only With Class Finding

Routine foot care includes nail trimming and debridement, corn and callus paring, and hygienic care of the feet. These services are excluded from Medicare coverage by default. They become covered when the patient has a systemic condition — the "Class Finding" — that makes it hazardous for a non-professional to perform the care.

The most common qualifying conditions are diabetes mellitus with peripheral neuropathy or vascular disease, peripheral arterial disease (PAD), peripheral neuropathy from any cause, and chronic venous insufficiency with skin changes. The podiatrist must document the systemic condition AND its specific manifestation in the feet (reduced sensation, poor circulation, skin fragility) that creates the hazard.

For patients: If you have diabetes, neuropathy, or poor circulation in your feet, your routine foot care (nail trimming, callus removal) is likely covered by Medicare. Tell your podiatrist about these conditions — they need to document them to bill Medicare for your care. Without this documentation, you'll be billed out of pocket even if you qualify for coverage.

The Q Modifier System

When routine foot care is covered through a Class Finding, the podiatrist must add a Q modifier to the claim indicating the qualifying condition category. Q7 indicates one Class A finding (the strongest — non-traumatic amputation, peripheral vascular disease, or peripheral neuropathy). Q8 indicates two Class B findings. Q9 indicates one Class B and two Class C findings. The specific modifier tells Medicare which systemic condition justifies the routine care coverage.

How Often Is Routine Foot Care Covered?

Medicare does not specify a hard frequency limit, but most MACs consider 60-day intervals to be the standard for routine foot care visits. Visits more frequently than every 60 days require additional documentation explaining why the shorter interval is medically necessary — such as rapidly growing mycotic nails or recurrent ulcer-prone calluses.

What If You Don't Qualify?

If you don't have a qualifying systemic condition, routine foot care is your responsibility to pay out of pocket. Typical self-pay costs for routine podiatry are $75-$150 per visit. Some podiatrists offer discounted cash-pay rates for routine care patients. Medicare Advantage plans may offer expanded routine foot care coverage — check your specific MA plan benefits.

The Bottom Line

Medicare covers foot care generously when it's medical, and conditionally when it's routine. The Class Finding is the dividing line — if you have diabetes, neuropathy, or vascular disease affecting your feet, routine care becomes a covered benefit. If you don't, it's out of pocket. For patients, the key action is making sure your podiatrist knows about your systemic conditions. For providers, the key action is documenting Class Findings thoroughly at every visit.
