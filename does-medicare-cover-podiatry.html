Does Medicare Cover Podiatry? Complete Coverage Guide

By PodiatryBillingClarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

Yes — Medicare covers podiatry services, but with an important distinction that affects nearly every claim: Medicare covers medically necessary podiatric care but does not cover routine foot care. This routine vs. medical distinction is the most critical billing concept in podiatry and the most common reason for claim denials and audit findings.

What Medicare Covers

Medical Foot Care (Covered)

Medicare Part B covers podiatric services that are medically necessary to diagnose or treat a foot condition. Covered services include treatment of foot injuries (fractures, sprains, wounds), surgical procedures (bunionectomy, hammertoe correction, neuroma excision), treatment of infections (fungal nails when causing secondary conditions, cellulitis, osteomyelitis), diabetic foot care (comprehensive diabetic foot exams, ulcer treatment, preventive care for high-risk diabetic feet), treatment of systemic conditions affecting the feet (peripheral vascular disease, neuropathy, arthritis), and evaluation and management of foot pain, deformity, or dysfunction.

Routine Foot Care with Medical Justification (Conditionally Covered)

This is where the complexity lives. Routine foot care — nail trimming, callus removal, corn paring — is normally excluded from Medicare coverage. However, it becomes covered when the patient has a systemic condition that creates a hazard if the care were performed by a non-professional. This is called the "Class Finding" requirement.

Qualifying systemic conditions include diabetes mellitus with peripheral neuropathy or vascular disease, peripheral vascular disease, peripheral neuropathy, chronic venous insufficiency, and other conditions documented in the Medicare Benefit Policy Manual that impair circulation or sensation to the feet.

The Class Finding is everything: Without a documented Class Finding connecting the patient's systemic condition to the need for professional foot care, routine services will be denied. Your chart must document the systemic diagnosis, the specific manifestation in the feet (neuropathy, vascular disease, skin fragility), and why a non-professional performing the care would create a hazard to the patient.

What Medicare Does NOT Cover

  • Routine foot care without a qualifying condition — Nail trimming, callus removal, and corn paring for patients without a systemic condition that creates a hazard
  • Flat foot treatment — Medicare specifically excludes treatment of flat feet unless it involves acute injury or secondary conditions
  • Supportive devices for flat feet — Arch supports and orthotic insoles for flat feet are not covered (except through TDSP for diabetics)
  • Hygienic foot care — Cleaning, soaking, and cosmetic treatment of toenails

How Much Does Medicare Pay?

Medicare pays 80% of the allowed amount for covered podiatry services after the Part B deductible (57 in 2026). The patient pays 20% coinsurance. Common podiatry services and approximate Medicare allowed amounts include E/M visits (99212-99215) at 5-50, nail debridement 11720 (1-5 nails) at 0-0, nail debridement 11721 (6+ nails) at 5-0, callus paring 11055 at 5-5, callus paring 11056 (2-4 lesions) at 0-5, and surgical procedures varying by complexity.

The Q7-Q9 Modifiers

For routine foot care billed with a Class Finding, podiatrists must append the appropriate Q modifier to indicate the qualifying systemic condition. Q7 indicates one Class A finding (non-traumatic amputation, peripheral vascular disease, or peripheral neuropathy requiring skilled foot care). Q8 indicates two Class B findings (absent posterior tibial pulse, absent dorsalis pedis pulse, etc.). Q9 indicates one Class B and two Class C findings. Using the correct Q modifier tells Medicare that the routine service is medically justified by the patient's systemic condition.

Documentation Requirements

Every Medicare podiatry claim must be supported by documentation of the patient's diagnosis and medical necessity, for routine foot care the Class Finding with specific systemic condition and foot manifestation, the specific service performed with clinical detail, and the patient's response and any complications or findings.

For patients receiving ongoing routine foot care (nail debridement, callus paring), the Class Finding must be reassessed and documented at least annually. A standing order to "trim nails every 60 days" without updated clinical assessment at each visit will not survive an audit.

Common Audit Triggers

  • High volume of routine foot care codes relative to medical/surgical codes
  • Missing or inadequate Class Findings — the systemic condition and foot manifestation aren't documented
  • Identical visit notes across multiple patients or visits
  • Billing 11721 (6+ nails) when fewer than 6 nails were actually debrided
  • Missing Q modifiers on routine foot care claims

The Bottom Line

Medicare covers a wide range of podiatric services — from surgical procedures to routine nail care for qualifying patients. The key to getting paid and staying compliant is understanding the routine vs. medical distinction, documenting Class Findings thoroughly for routine foot care, and using the correct modifiers on every claim. The OIG has identified podiatry billing as an active audit priority, making documentation accuracy more important than ever.
